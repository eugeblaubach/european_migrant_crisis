European Migrant Crisis

A visual story by Eugenia Blaubach

Begin

For some, Europe’s migrant crisis evokes images like this one: an overcrowded boat carrying Syrian and Iraqi refugees across the Mediterranean Sea.

Image by Ggia

For others, it conjures up memories of Europe’s recent terrorist attacks, heightens concerns about national security and sparks political debate over the level of access migrants should be granted to the region.

Image by David Holt

The UNHCR estimates that 362,000 refugees and migrants crossed the Mediterranean Sea in 2016.

A large majority of those individuals were fleeing war-torn countries like Syria, Iraq and Afghanistan where Islam is the dominant religion.

Image by Mstyslav Chernov

The influx of migrants has been met by a surge of racist, nationalist sentiments throughout Europe.

According to a 2016 Pew Research Center study, negative opinions about Muslims have increased in Poland, Spain, France, Germany and the UK since 2015.

Image by Silar

However, recent refugees make up a small percentage of Europe's Muslim population.

People from Islamic nations have been migrating to Europe since the end of World War II. By 1970, there were already an estimated 19 million Muslims living in the region.

Image by Cramos

The apprehension Europeans feel toward refugees today is fueled in part by their unfavorable views of Muslims that had already been living in the region.

In fact, the Pew Research Center found that Europeans who have preexisting unfavorable views of Muslims are more likely to say refugees will increase the likelihood of terrorism in their country.

Image by Marko Kudjerski

The Root of Intolerance

Estimating the size of Europe’s Muslim population is difficult. Some European countries, like France, do not measure religion in the national census. Other countries, like Germany, attempt to measure it, but the results are largely inaccurate. Consequently, most estimates are based on country of origin.

This is where the debate lies. Country of origin, or race for that matter, does not denote religion. A white European with an all-German lineage could be Muslim. A second-generation Algerian with Arab features living in France can be Catholic. However, when both of these individuals apply for a job, who is more likely to be hired?

Stéphane Charbonnier, the late editor in chief of the French satirical magazine Charlie Hebdo, argues that what has been widely referred to as Islamophobia or xenophobia is in fact racism.

“The fact that racists may also be Islamophobic is essentially incidental. They are racists first, and merely use Islam to target their intended victim,” Charbonnier said.

To fully understand Europe's current migrant crisis, it’s essential to look back at previous waves of migration.

France and Germany have the largest Muslim populations in the region. Let's take a closer look at the historical influx of immigrants to those countries.

Image by ondasderuido

FRANCE

{1850s - 1980s}

During the industrial revolution and after World War I, France sought out migrant workers to meet the demands of the labor market.

Those who migrated to France between 1850 and 1945 originated mostly from neighboring countries, such as Italy, Switzerland and Poland.

The majority of them were white Roman Catholics who had to make minor changes to assimilate to French life.

Image by Agence Rol

After World War II, France began to experience a 30-year period of economic prosperity known as the Trente Glorieuses. The war had left the country with a dwindling workforce and low birthrates, so immigration was encouraged.

Starting in the 1950s, the source of immigration shifted from European countries to North African countries, particularly Algeria.

According to The World Factbook, 99 percent of Algerians identify as Arab Muslims. Unlike the previous waves of migration, Algerians brought over other socio-cultural characteristics that made assimilating a harder task.

Image by Agence Dalmas

France and Algeria

Algeria became French colony in 1830. After World War II, tensions with the colony heightened, giving rise to the Algerian nationalist movement. The Algerian War for Independence broke out in 1954 and Algeria gained independence in 1962.

This troubling period in history, marked by political turmoil and economic uncertainty, led thousands of Algerians to migrate to France. The Migration Policy Institute estimates that from 1968 to 1982, the Algerian immigrant population in France nearly doubled from around 470,000 to 800,000.

In 1973, the oil crisis shook the world economy, ending the country's Trente Glorieuses and plunging it into a prolonged economic crisis. The country saw a quick and steady rise in unemployment and inflation.

To cope with the crisis, President Giscard d'Estaing imposed unpopular austerity measures. In July 1974, the government ended labor recruitment efforts in North Africa.

Image by BeenAroundAWhile

Around this time, France saw the emergence of the National Front, a far-right nationalist political party.

Jean Marie Le Pen, the founder, capitalized on the nation’s growing discontent to fuel racist, anti-immigrant sentiments.

The country has since had a rightward shift in immigration policy, creating unfavorable conditions for immigrants.

Image by Marie-Lan Nguyen

GERMANY

{1960s - 1990s}

In the late 1950s, post-war labor shortages prompted West Germany to recruit temporary foreign workers to fill labor gaps in the mining and manufacturing sectors.

The country recruited guest worker by signing bilateral recruitment agreements with Italy, Spain and Greece as well as multiple Muslim nations including Turkey, Morocco and Tunisia.

Guest workers intended to travel to West Germany, work for a few years and then return home.

Image by the U.S. National Archives and Records Administration

Much like in France, recruitment of foreign guest workers continued until the 1973 oil crisis that plunged the country into a recession.

By that time, Turkey had surpassed Italy as the most prominent source of immigration. According to the Migration Policy Institute, Turkish people accounted for 23 percent of foreigners in Germany in 1973.

Although they were considered guest workers, many Turks remained in Germany after the halt of foreign recruitment. Their families later joined them to permanently settle in the country.

Image by Heinrich Klaffs

Prior to the 20th century, Germany had not received such large wave of migrants. It considered itself a country of emigration not immigration.

The West German government was not prepared to handle the influx of families. Specific policies for guest workers had not been created, and integration programs had not been set in place.

Image by Heinrich Klaffs

As questions about the country's national identity rose, so did racist, anti-Muslim sentiments.

Almost 30 years later, the same questions reverberate throughout the nation.

Image by Heinrich Klaffs

Immigrants have been contributing new skills and knowledge to German and French societies for decades now.

They've strengthened the labor force and made each nation more diverse and pluralistic.

Yet, political parties in the far right have gained significant popularity in both France and Germany.

Image by Arne List

Germany’s nationalist party, Alternative for Germany, became the third largest party in the federal parliament in 2017.

The AFD is the country's first far-right party in parliament since the 1960s.

Image by Kürschner

Marine le Pen, the daughter of Jean Marie le Pen and current leader of the National Front, came in second place during the 2017 French presidential election.

Image by Blandine Le Cain
